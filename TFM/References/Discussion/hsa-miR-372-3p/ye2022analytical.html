Analytical Validation and Performance Characteristics of Molecular Serum Biomarkers, miR-371a-3p and miR-372-3p, for Male Germ Cell Tumors, in a Clinical Laboratory Setting

Author links open overlay panel, , , , , , , , , ,
https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jmoldx.2022.04.007Get rights and content
Under an Elsevier user license
open archive
Detection of serum embryonic miRNAs miR-371a-3p and miR-372-3p has been proposed to aid in diagnosis, prognosis, and management of patients with testicular germ cell tumors (GCTs). This study describes the analytical validation and performance of a laboratory-developed test to detect these miRNA targets by stem loop real-time quantitative RT-PCR (RT-qPCR) in serum from patients with GCTs. The assay was standardized using an exogenous spike-in control of nonhuman miRNA from Caenorhabditis elegans (cel-miR-39-3p) to assess extraction efficiency, and an endogenous housekeeping miRNA, miR-30b-5p, to control for miRNA normalization. miRNA results were expressed as relative expression level, using the comparative threshold cycle method (2ΔΔCT). Analytical sensitivity of miR-371a-3p and miR-372-3p was 12.5 and 1.25 copies/μL, respectively. Clinical accuracy was evaluated using GCT patients with (n = 34) and without (n = 17) active disease. Positive/negative cutoffs and indeterminate findings were established on the basis of results from healthy volunteers (n = 25) and assay precision. miR-371a-3p and miR-372-3p exhibited a sensitivity of 81.8% and 87.5%, respectively, and a specificity of 100% and 94%, respectively, and an area under the receiver operating characteristic curve of 0.93 and 0.95, respectively. Taken together, RT-qPCR testing for serum miR-371a-3p and miR-372-3p represents a robust, sensitive, and specific clinical assay to aid in the clinical management of patients with GCT.

miRNAs are small noncoding RNAs that function in the regulation of gene expression1 and may be dysregulated in cancer.2,3 Indeed, miRNAs have been shown to participate in carcinogenesis by modulating cell proliferation, resisting cell death, activating invasion and metastasis, and inducing angiogenesis.2 Evidence is accumulating to support the concept that miRNA signatures may serve as diagnostic and prognostic biomarkers and may also predict therapy response.4
Germ cell tumors (GCTs) are a heterogeneous group of neoplasms originating at various points in germ cell development.5 Depending on disease stage and tumor histology, GCTs are treated with surgery, followed by radiation and/or chemotherapy.6,7 Significant long-term effects of systemic therapy have been observed and, therefore, early and accurate diagnosis is important to achieve optimal clinical outcomes.8
Guidelines for diagnosis, prognostic assessment, and management of patients with GCTs include evaluation of serum biomarkers, α-fetoprotein (AFP), human chorionic gonadotropin (HCG), and lactate dehydrogenase (LDH).9 These serum markers, however, lack sensitivity and specificity for GCTs10,11 and are less informative in patients with seminoma, teratoma, and embryonal carcinoma, which represent a large proportion of GCT patients.6 Recently, the presence of serum embryonic miRNA clusters, miR-371 to miR-373 and miR-302/367, in patients with GCT has been described.12, 13, 14, 15, 16 Of the two miRNA clusters, miR-371a-3p appears the most sensitive and specific, with an area under the receiver operating characteristic curve of >0.90, a sensitivity of 89% to 96%, and a specificity of >90% for both seminoma and nonseminoma.17 Results of a multicenter study indicate that detection of serum miR-371a-3p outperforms the sensitivity and specificity of standard-of-care serum biochemical markers in this setting.14 However, the sensitivity of this marker for detection of relapse is decreased to 82.6%.14 Furthermore, miR-371a-3p alone was not sufficient to identify some malignant GCTs.18 Moreover, the particularly rapid reduction in miR-371a-3p levels following tumor removal in stage 1 disease19,20 will lead to negative results in samples taken >1 day after surgery, unlike the other miRNAs, such as miR-372-3p, which has a longer half-life.18 Furthermore, of the miR-371 to miR-373 cluster (miR-371, miR-372, and miR-373), serum level of miR-372-3p was shown to be significantly elevated at the time of diagnosis of a pediatric yolk sac tumor.21 Functionally, both miR-372-3p and miR-373-3p contribute to cancer development in the testis by disabling the p53 pathway via targeting of the LATS2 tumor suppressor gene, although the full pathway has yet to be elucidated.17 Potentially, more than one miRNA biomarker can be subject to considerable variation in levels for technical as well as biological reasons. In addition, combination of miRNA markers resulted in higher sensitivity and higher specificity compared with an individual marker.16 Therefore, the two miRNAs (miR-371a-3p and miR-372-3p) were included in this study.
Clinical application of miRNA testing in patients with GCTs is being considered, requiring the evaluation of analytical performance outside of research settings.22 Analytical performance of miRNA testing requires a high degree of technical expertise and robust protocols and procedures that address specimen collection, RNA extraction, reverse transcriptase PCR measurement, result interpretation, and reporting.23 Major sources of analytical variability are associated with RNA isolation methods,24 preanalytical sample processing,25 including sample storage and hemolysis,25,26 as well as quantitative PCR (qPCR) measurement systems,27 and data processing and normalization.10 The present study describes the validation of a clinical laboratory-developed test for the detection of miR-371a-3p and miR-372-3p in serum from patients with GCTs, using stem loop real-time quantitative RT-PCR (RT-qPCR), with a focus on analytical performance, including sensitivity, specificity, precision, accuracy, and result interpretation.

Materials and Methods

Blood Specimen Collection and Processing

Blood specimens were collected from patients with a history of testicular GCTs, including patients with and without active disease. Disease status was determined on the basis of clinical assessment, biochemical markers, and radiological studies. The patient population consisted mainly of individuals with nonseminomatous tumors and relapsed and/or metastatic disease. Patient demographics are summarized in Table 1. Informed consent for blood collection was obtained under a Memorial Sloan Kettering (FWA: 00004998) approved institutional review board protocol (institutional review board number 15-220). Deidentified blood specimens from healthy adult volunteers (n = 25) were obtained to establish reference range cutoffs. The healthy adult volunteers were of the same ages and consisted of 30% male and 70% female. There was no difference in miRNA expression level whether the sample was from a male or a female donor (data not shown).

Whole blood was collected into gold-top blood collection tubes (SST; Becton Dickinson, Franklin Lakes, NJ) between January 2020 and April 2021. Samples were allowed to clot for at least 30 minutes at room temperature. Subsequently and within 4 hours of blood collection, samples were centrifuged at room temperature (1900 × g for 15 minutes). The supernatant serum was removed, aliquoted, and stored at −80°C until use. Frozen serum aliquots were thawed on ice before RNA extraction.

RNA Extraction

Total RNA, including small RNAs, was extracted from 200 μL of thawed serum using the miRNeasy Serum/Plasma Advanced kit (Qiagen, Germantown, MD; catalog number 217204), according to manufacturer's instructions. To measure miRNA extraction efficiency, a nonhuman spike-in Caenorhabditis elegans (cel)-miR-39-3p oligonucleotide control was added to each serum sample at a fixed quantity (5.6 × 108 copies) before RNA extraction, as per the manufacturer's instruction. Total RNA was eluted in 20 μL of nuclease-free water and stored at −80°C until further use.

Stem-Loop RT-qPCR (TaqMan Assay)

Specific miRNA levels were determined using stem-loop RT-qPCR, as described previously.14,18 Briefly, a fixed amount of extracted total RNA (5 μL per reaction) was reverse transcribed into cDNA using the TaqMan MicroRNA Reverse Transcription Kit (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Waltham, MA; catalog number 4366596) in singleplex. TaqMan assays were used for cDNA synthesis and quantification of miRNAs, including miR-371a-3p, miR-372-3p, and cel-miR-39-3p. In addition, endogenous cellular markers, miR-30b-5p and hsa-miR-451a, were evaluated as internal monitors of RT-qPCR efficiency, and to assess the effect of hemolysis, respectively. TaqMan assays and miRNA sequences used in reverse transcription, pre-amplification, and qPCR are detailed in Table 2.

The reverse transcription reaction mixture was incubated in a thermocycler at 16°C for 30 minutes and 42°C for 30 minutes, followed by 85°C for 5 minutes. The resulting cDNA was pre-amplified using 2 × TaqMan Preamp master mix and selected miRNA primers. Pre-amplification occurred in a thermocycler with 1 cycle each of 95°C for 10 minutes, 55°C for 2 minutes, and 72°C for 2 minutes, followed by 14 cycles each of 95°C for 15 seconds, and 60°C for 4 minutes. The pre-amplification product was diluted 1:8 in nuclease-free water and taken into qPCR using 2 × TaqMan Fast Advanced master mix and miRNA primers and probes shown in Table 2, using a QuantStudio 6 Flex Real-Time PCR instrument (Thermo Fisher Scientific). The qPCR cycling conditions were 95°C for 10 minutes, followed by 40 cycles of 95°C for 15 seconds and 60°C for 60 seconds. ΔCT(sample) = CT(target miRNA) − CT(Corrected miR-30b-5p)
CT(Corrected miR-30b-5p) = CT(miR-30b-5p) – (CT(cel-miR-39-3p sample) − CT(cel-miR-39-3p lowest in same run))
This formula corrects for RNA extraction and amplification variations by subtracting the lowest within-run CT value of the exogenous control, cel-miR-39-3p, from the other cel-miR-39-3p CT levels of the samples in the same run. The formula additionally normalizes the sample to its endogenously expressed housekeeping miRNA, miR-30b-5p, as well as to a nontumor control group (ΔCT(healthy donor)). The ΔCT of the nontumor control group was calculated using the average CT value of each target obtained from the 25 healthy donors.

Statistical Analysis

Assay performance was evaluated using GraphPad Prism 9.2.0 (GraphPad Inc., San Diego, CA) for receiver operating characteristic analysis and by determining clinical sensitivity and specificity, as well as positive and negative predictive values.29 Calculations were based on miR-371a-3p and miR-372-3p results (relative expression levels and cutoffs) compared with the clinical outcome (active disease versus nonactive disease). Clinical outcome was defined by the clinical oncologist based on the results of serum tumor markers and radiographic evidence.

Results

Assay Standardization

Several quality control steps were incorporated into the assay to ensure robust serum RT-qPCR detection and relative quantification. The exogenous nonhuman spike-in control, cel-miR-39-3p, was used to monitor miRNA extraction efficiency. An endogenous housekeeping miRNA, miR-30b-5p, reported to be highly stable in serum,16,18,30 was used to control for miRNA load and normalization purposes. For further quality control and to monitor contamination, each RT-qPCR run included a negative control (healthy donor serum) and a nontemplate control.
Figure 1 shows results for amplification of cel-miR-39-3p and the housekeeping miR-30b-5p in 76 serum samples (25 from healthy donors, and 51 from GCT patients). CT values, 15.5 ± 3.6 (mean ± 3 SDs), for the exogenous miRNA cel-miR-39-3p were consistent across samples, with a CV of 7.5%, indicating acceptable miRNA extraction efficiency and reproducibility.31 Similarly, CT values, 19.4 ± 4.8 (mean ± 3 SDs), were highly reproducible for the endogenous miRNA control, miR-30b-5p, used for normalization, with a CV of 8.4%. A single sample (Figure 1) demonstrated out-of-range CT values for both cel-miR-39-3p and miR-30b-5p, indicating that miRNA extraction efficiency was poor. When miRNA re-extraction was performed, CT values of 16.8 and 21.1 for cel-miR-39-3p and miR-30b-5p, respectively, were within acceptable limits. These findings suggested that miR-30b-5p is abundant and stably expressed in serum from healthy donors and patients with GCTs. On the basis of these results, acceptable control ranges were set at CT values of 15 to 24 and 12 to 19 for cel-miR-39-3p and miR-30b-5p, respectively, representing a 3-SD range.

Assay Performance

Linearity

The linear range for miR-371a-3p and miR-372-3p detection was determined using two synthetic oligonucleotides specific to miR-371a-3p and miR-372-3p, which were serially diluted from 1.25 × 106 copies/μL using nuclease-free water. In addition, two serum samples from patients with active disease were serially diluted using serum from a healthy donor in ratios of 1:10, 1:100, 1:1000, and 1:10,000. Each dilution was assayed using the stem loop real-time RT-qPCR for the two miRNAs (miR-371a-3p and miR-372-3p) in three independent runs. The results are summarized in Figure 2. The data demonstrate excellent linearity for both miRNAs, miR-371a-3p and miR-372-3p, over a wide range of copy numbers.

PCR Efficiency

The lowest PCR efficiency observed across three runs was 86%. PCR efficiency depends on many factors, such as: assay performance (which depends on primer and template sequences and structures); sample matrix (which may contain inhibitors and other interfering substances); reagents used; and short miRNA targets. Therefore, on the basis of the resultant data (representative data shown in Figure 2) and generally accepted principles of good laboratory practice, a PCR efficiency range of 85% to 115% was considered acceptable for stem-loop RT-PCR for miRNA GCT testing.

Analytical Sensitivity

To determine analytical sensitivity, two synthetic oligonucleotides specific for miR-371a-3p and miR-372-3p, with known copy numbers, were serially diluted 10-fold from 12,500 to 0.0125 copies/μL using nucleic acid-free water. Each dilution was analyzed in triplicate in three independent runs. The results are shown in Table 3. The analytical sensitivity for miR-371a-3p was 12.5 copies/μL, and it was 1.25 copies/μL for miR-372-3p, with acceptable CVs of <10%.

Reproducibility

The within-run and between-run reproducibility was assessed by analyzing four clinical samples from GCT patients with active disease. The samples were analyzed in triplicate in three independent runs with a week interval between the runs. CT values of these four clinical samples ranged from 21 to 31 and 20 to 30 for miR-371a-3p and miR-372-3p, respectively. The within-assay CVs using CT values ranged from 1.2% to 2.8% and from 0.3% to 1.0% for miR-371a-3p and miR-372-3p, respectively. The interassay precision ranged from 0.3% to 3.7% and from 2.2% to 3.5% for miR-371a-3p and miR-372-3p, respectively (data not shown).
When CT values were normalized on the basis of exogenous nonhuman spike-in cel-miR-39-3p and endogenous housekeeping miRNA miR-30b-5p and results expressed as ΔCT values, the reproducibility between replicates was more variable. CVs ranged from 3.9% to 32% for miR-371a-3p, and from 6.5% to 20.8% for miR-372-3p, in the intra-assay precision studies (Figure 3A). The interassay precision ranged from 2.7% to 15% and from 2.2% to 31.5% for miR-371a-3p and miR-372-3p, respectively (Figure 3B). The increased variability of normalized expression levels relative to CT values is explained by the qPCR method principle,32 with a CT variability of up to one cycle, which can result in an error of up to 0.5 or 2 times the true value. The expected variability in relative expression for miR-371a-3p and miR-372-3p was taken into consideration during result interpretation, with the generation of an indeterminate category, which reflects variability around the target detection threshold.

Result Interpretation

To establish negative cutoff values for both CT and relative expression levels for miR-371a-3p and miR-372-3p using the stem loop real-time RT-qPCR assay, a total of 25 negative serum samples from healthy donors were analyzed. The cutoff CT values and relative expression levels were defined as mean ± SD (90% CI). CT value cutoffs of 34 and 30 and relative expression levels of 0.5 and 1.0 were determined for miR-371a-3p and miR-372-3p, respectively (data not shown).
An indeterminate category was established for miR-371a-3p and miR-372-3p based on the assay reproducibility. The indeterminate relative expression range for each target was calculated by applying the mean CV% for intra-assay variability to the relative expression cutoff. Thus, three result categories were established: negative, indeterminate, and positive. Relative expression levels of ≤0.49 and ≤0.99 were considered negative for miR-371a-3p and miR-372-3p, respectively. Relative expression levels of ≥0.75 and ≥1.5 were considered positive, for the respective targets. Values falling between these cutoffs were considered indeterminate.

Accuracy: Assay Sensitivity and Specificity

To determine the clinical accuracy of miR-371a-3p and miR-372-3p detection by the stem loop real-time RT-qPCR assay, a total of 51 clinical serum samples obtained during diagnosis, treatment, or follow-up from GCT patients were examined. These included 34 blood samples from patients with active disease and 17 blood samples from patients without active disease. Three patients contributed two samples each, obtained at different time points. Disease status was defined by the clinical oncologist (D.R.F.) based on results of standard serum tumor markers (HCG, AFP, and LDH) and radiographic evidence.
Using the relative expression cutoffs of ≥0.75 for miR-371a-3p and ≥1.5 for miR-372-3p, miR-371a-3p and miR-372-3p were detected in 27 and 28 samples from patients with active disease, respectively. None of the 17 samples from patients without active disease were positive for miR-371a-3p, but one sample was positive for miR-372-3p (Table 4 and Supplemental Table S1). These results suggest a clinical sensitivity of 81.8% or 87.5%, respectively, for miR-371a-3p and miR-372-3p, and clinical specificity of 100% and 94%, respectively (Table 5). Receiver operating characteristic analysis of miR-371a-3p and miR-372a-3p discriminated between patients with and without active disease with an area under the curve of ≥0.93 and ≥0.95, respectively (Figure 4). These results indicate that both miR-371a-3p and miR-372-3p are sensitive and specific biomarkers for testicular germ cell tumors.

The GCT patients were further stratified into two primary tumor site subgroups (41 from testis and 10 from mediastinum). Although the number of samples in primary mediastinal site is relatively small, miR-371a-3p and miR-372-3p were detected in seven of nine and eight of nine samples from mediastinal GCTs with active disease, respectively, and not detected in the one primary mediastinal patient without active disease, resulting in a clinical sensitivity of 77.8% or 88.9%, respectively, for miR-371a-3p and miR-372-3p, with a clinical specificity of 100% for both (Supplemental Tables S1–S3). The sensitivity for GCT with a testicular primary tumor site was 83.3% for miR-371a-3p, which is slightly higher than that for patients with a primary mediastinal GCT. The clinical specificity of miR-371a-3p for testicular GCTs was 100% (Supplemental Tables S1, S4, and S5). For miR-372-3p, the clinical sensitivity was 87% and specificity was 93.3% for testicular GCTs (Supplemental Tables S1, S4, and S5), which is similar to the sensitivity and specificity of miR-372-3p among patients with a mediastinal primary tumor site.
In addition, the sensitivity of the two miRNA markers was compared with traditional tumor markers currently used as the gold standard [AFP, beta HCG (bHCG), and LDH]. As expected, sensitivity is lower at 47.1%, 64.7%, and 52.9% when using AFP, bHCG, or LDH to correlate with patient disease status, respectively, and is consistent with other studies.10,12,13 The assay sensitivity established when using GCT miRNA targets, miR-371a-3p and miR-372-3p, demonstrated a drastic improvement in distinguishing patients with active disease from no active disease, compared with when using traditional biomarkers (AFP, bHCG, and LDH).

Preanalytical Variables

Specimen Stability

The stability of miR-371a-3p and miR-372-3p in whole blood was evaluated for up to 96 hours after blood collection to mimic clinical blood sample handling and transport to a central testing laboratory. Blood was stored at 4°C and processed to obtain serum at 4, 24, 48, 72, and 96 hours after blood collection. miRNA levels were stable for 72 hours after blood collection at 4°C (Figure 5). Stability was defined as a ΔCT value <0.5 when comparing the designated time point with the initial time point at 4 hours after blood collection.

Effect of Hemolysis

Because miR-30b-5p is highly expressed in red blood cells, the impact of sample hemolysis was examined. The data show that serum miR-30b-5p levels are higher in hemolyzed samples with a hemoglobin concentration of ≥120 mg/dL (Figure 6). This degree of hemolysis imparts a subtle red tinge of color to serum and correlates with increased hemolysis-dependent miR-451a levels.33 Expression of miR-371a-3p and miR-372-3p cannot be normalized accurately in the setting of hemolysis and will result in an underestimation of the relative expression level for these molecular markers. This is particularly relevant for samples with negative or indeterminate results. A result comment indicating this limitation will thus be included in future patient reports, as well as a specific notation for samples that are visibly hemolyzed.

Discussion

The data show that stem-loop RT-qPCR testing for miR-371a-3p and miR-372-3p in the serum of patients with history of GCTs can be performed accurately and reliably in a clinical laboratory setting, with appropriate quality control procedures. Following stringent preanalytical, analytical, and postanalytical procedures, the sensitivity and specificity of miRNA miR-371a-3p and miR-372-3p testing showed good correlation with the presence or absence of disease among patients with GCTs. These findings support previous reports13,14 and extend findings to patients with mediastinal primary GCTs and more extensive treatment.
Interestingly, in the current study, miR-372-3p showed a higher sensitivity than miR-371a-3p (87.5% versus 81.8%; areas under the curve of 0.95 and 0.93, respectively), which contradicts the results of Dieckmann et al,13 which indicated that miR-371a-3p was the most sensitive GCT serum biomarker among the miR-371 to miR-373 and miR-302/367 clusters. However, those studies included few patients with metastases or relapsed disease, whereas in the current study, most patients had relapsed disease and metastatic disease at the time of sample collection. Moreover, this study differs from others because the comparison control group consisted of GCT patients without active disease as opposed to prior publications that used healthy patients or those with non-GCT malignancies as controls. Therefore, these findings must be assessed with caution because miRNA level may not be the same among healthy control, non-GCT control, and GCT patients without active disease.14,15 In addition, the current study showed a sensitivity of 81.8% for miR-371a-3p, which is lower than the 90.1% in the primary GCT subgroup but consistent with a sensitivity of only 82.6% in the subgroup of relapsing cases reported in the largest study so far.14 It is possible that the lower sensitivity of miR-371a-3p in this assay is a result of including mostly patients with relapsed disease as well as the inclusion of some with primary mediastinal GCTs (n = 10), and the sensitivity of which may be somewhat lower in these populations.
Previous studies of circulating miRNAs for patients with the primary mediastinal GCT malignant-GCT subtype has been exceptionally limited.34 A separate analysis was performed to compare mediastinal GCT test results of miRNAs miR-371a-3p and miR-372-3p with those of the testicular GCT test results, which indicated that the sensitivity for GCT with primary mediastinal GCT is slightly lower than those in testicular primary tumor site. One study34 identified a panel of C19MC miRNAs (chromosome 19 miRNA cluster, or miR-515 to miR-526) that were highly elevated in one patient with a widely metastatic primary mediastinal nonseminomatous GCT at diagnosis, and confirmed elevation of the established miRNA marker miR-371a-3p34, which is consistent with the current findings.
Both miRNA biomarkers showed an excellent positive predictive value. However, the negative predictive value of <80% of either miRNA alone or both together indicates the possibility of false-negative results. Depending on the intended clinical use, the reference range cutoffs could be adjusted to eliminate the chance of false-negative results, at the cost of increasing the false positivity rate. To improve result reliability, assay reproducibility was considered in the interpretation of miRNA results, such that result interpretation included three categories: target detected, target not detected, and indeterminate. In addition, the data suggest that RNA should be re-extracted if the CT value for cel-miR-39-3p is out of acceptable range, and a new specimen requested if the CT value for miR-30b-5p is out of acceptable range. To further improve assay performance for samples with low levels of biomarkers, both CT value and relative expression levels for each miRNA are used together during result interpretation. Samples with mismatched positive/negative CT value and relative expression level should be re-extracted and reanalyzed.
To achieve robust and reproducible quantification of miRNAs, normalization against at least two reference miRNAs, which are present at stable levels across the sample, should be used to correct for variations in sample composition, RNA extraction, RNA handling and storage, RNA load, and the RT-qPCR protocol. In the current protocol, a spiked-in C. elegans miRNA control and an endogenous housekeeping gene were used to fulfill this requirement, and results were expressed as relative expression levels, rather than raw CT values. In addition, each analytical run included a negative control, a serum sample from a healthy volunteer, and a positive control, representing a previously analyzed positive patient sample. Challenges with incorporating a positive assay control into each run included the lack of commercially available cell lines expressing the desired miRNAs and the risk of contamination with use of specific oligonucleotides. In addition, a no target control was included to detect contamination, and any sample without target amplification was repeated to control for manual testing errors.
Most of the highly expressed miRNAs in serum are derived from blood cells and are released into serum following cell lysis.35 Therefore, the effect of hemolysis on target miRNAs was evaluated. Not only did hemoglobin interfere with PCR, but the results indicate that hemolysis-susceptible reference miRNA (miR-30b-5p) will impact the determination of relative target expression levels. As reported previously, samples that were visually hemolyzed showed decreased target relative expression levels.26 Therefore, the inclusion of an interpretive comment with results when a serum sample is visually hemolyzed to indicate the potential reduction in relative expression is recommended. This would be most relevant for samples testing negative for miR-371a-3p and miR-372-3p or samples with indeterminate results. The use of an alternative reference miRNA could also be considered. For example, Myklebust et al26 reported that miRNA miR-191-5p represents a relatively stable alternative reference miRNA in serum samples with mild hemolysis.
Patients with GCTs require intensive follow-up after diagnosis and treatment. Unlike the serum protein markers AFP, β-HCG, and LDH, which are not uniformly elevated in patients with GCT, are significantly associated with tumor histology, clinical and pathologic stages, tumor size, and younger age, and may change on relapse,7,9, 10, 11 evaluation of serum miR-371a-3p and miR-372-3p represents a noninvasive method with potentially greater sensitivity and specificity.12,22 The integration of serum miRNA analysis into the evaluation of patients with GCTs, using validated clinical laboratory processes and procedures, could thus have significant positive impact on clinical trials and the diagnosis and management of patients with GCTs.17,36 This study demonstrates that serum miR-371a-3p and miR-372-3p analysis can be validated for clinical applications and that prospective studies should be initiated to determine whether miRNA monitoring during GCT treatment and follow-up care can improve patient outcomes.

  • Perioperative Serum MicroRNA 371a-3p and 372-3p Levels in Patients with Clinically Localized Testicular Masses

    2024, European Urology Open Science
    Citation Excerpt :

    Our previously described combination assay (miR-371a-3p and miR-372-3p, collectively Memorial Sloan Kettering Cancer Center [MSK] miRNA assay [MMA]) was created based on the prevailing performance characteristics of miR-371a-3p and the potential benefit of miR-372-3p. The extent literature suggests that miR-371a-3p alone demonstrates lower sensitivity in the detection of relapse; miR-372-3p has been shown to be markedly elevated in pediatric yolk sac tumors and has a longer half-life, and therefore was selected for inclusion in our assay [15]. We sought to study diagnostic test characteristics of our combined assay in patients with localized testicular masses to determine possible marginal benefit of this approach, to determine what patient and tumor factors may influence levels of these markers, and to explore postorchiectomy kinetics.

    MicroRNAs (miRNAs) show promise as blood-based tumor markers for germ cell tumors (GCTs), with miRNA-371-3p being the most studied. The marginal benefit of including other candidate miRNAs to aid with the management of testicular GCTs remains unclear.
    To assess the performance of our combined miRNA assay (371a-3p and 372-3p) in patients with clinically localized testicular masses.
    This was a retrospective review of patients prospectively enrolled in an ongoing protocol collecting serum miR-371a-3p and miR-372-3p levels (together, Memorial Sloan Kettering Cancer Center [MSK] miRNA assay [MMA]) in patients with a suspected or diagnosed testicular GCT.
    The coprimary outcomes of interest were sensitivity and specificity of miR-371a-3p and 372-3p, individually and together, to detect nonteratomatous GCTs in the orchiectomy specimen. Secondary outcomes included additional assay diagnostic parameters, the relationship of patient and disease factors with variations in miRNA levels, and temporal patterns of miRNA normalization after orchiectomy.
    Sixty-two patients were included, 52 had a viable GCT at orchiectomy, and ten had no cancer or a non-GCT. Forty-six patients with a GCT had positive preorchiectomy MMA (sensitivity 88.5% [95% confidence interval {CI}: 79.8, 97.2]), and one patient had positive preorchiectomy MMA but no GCT (specificity 90.0% [95% CI: 71.4, 100]). The diagnostic performance of miR-371a-3-p and miR-372-3p was similar. The time for miRNA to decrease to undetectable levels varied, with some patients having positive levels up to 3 wk after orchiectomy.
    The biomarkers miR-371a-3p and miR-372-3p demonstrated high sensitivity and specificity for localized testicular GCTs, but causes of variation in relative miRNA levels and time to normalization for individual patients remain unclear.
    We studied the ability of the blood-based biomarkers miR-371a-3p and miR-372-3p to detect testicular cancer (germ cell tumors) in patients with small testicular masses. We found that together and individually these were sensitive and specific for testicular cancer.
